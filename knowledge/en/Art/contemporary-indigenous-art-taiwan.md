# Contemporary Indigenous Art in Taiwan

Taiwanese indigenous contemporary art has undergone a remarkable transformation over the past 30 years. Since the 1990s, a group of indigenous artists began leaving their tribal communities to enter the mainstream art world, using their creative work to redefine what constitutes "indigenous art." They are no longer merely custodians of traditional crafts but active creators engaging with contemporary issues, showcasing the rich dimensions of Taiwanese indigenous culture on the international art stage.

## From Traditional Crafts to Contemporary Transformation

### Traditional Crafts as Artistic Foundation

Each of Taiwan's indigenous groups possesses sophisticated traditional craft techniques that serve as vital nourishment for contemporary creation. The weaving of the Atayal (泰雅族), wood carving of the Paiwan (排灣族), pottery of the Amis (阿美族), and boat-building crafts of the Tao (達悟族) — each technique embodies profound cultural significance and aesthetic principles.

Atayal weaving stands as the most refined craft. Traditional floor loom textiles feature geometric patterns like diamonds and stripes, each with specific names and meanings. The "Eyes of Ancestors" motif represents ancestral protection, while "Sunbeams" symbolizes life force. These patterns are not merely decorative but carriers of collective memory.

Paiwan wood and stone carving techniques are equally magnificent. The hundred-pace viper (百步蛇), human head, and sun motifs represent classic elements reflecting Paiwan reverence for nature and ancestors. Traditional woodcarvings adorned chiefs' houses, displaying aristocratic status and social hierarchy.

While these traditional skills face succession crises in the contemporary era, they simultaneously provide rich materials and inspiration for modern artistic creation. Many indigenous artists reinterpret these traditional elements in their work, breathing new life into ancient crafts within modern contexts.

### Transition from Traditional to Contemporary

In the late 1980s, Taiwanese society began valuing multiculturalism, and indigenous art gradually gained attention. The 1988 "Taiwan Indigenous Art Festival" marked an important starting point for indigenous art entering mainstream art systems. This event not only showcased traditional crafts but also encouraged innovative creation.

During this period, some indigenous artists began receiving modern art education, learning Western artistic techniques like oil painting, sculpture, and printmaking. They contemplated how to combine traditional culture with modern art during their studies, initiating explorations of "traditional craft contemporization."

This transition process was not smooth. Early works were often questioned as being "not traditional enough" or "not contemporary enough." Artists had to find balance between maintaining cultural authenticity and pursuing artistic innovation. Through years of exploration, Taiwanese indigenous contemporary art gradually developed its distinctive characteristics and vocabulary.

## Prominent Artists and Creative Journeys

### First-Generation Pioneers

**Sakinu Pawavalung (撒古流‧巴瓦瓦隆)** stands as an important pioneer of Taiwanese indigenous contemporary art. This Paiwan artist began creating in the 1980s, combining traditional Paiwan wood carving techniques with contemporary installation art concepts. His "Ancestral House Series" transforms traditional house concepts into large-scale installation works, recreating sacred Paiwan spaces within museum environments.

Sakinu's creation extends beyond artistic expression to cultural revitalization practice. He established a workshop in Sandimen, Pingtung County, cultivating wood carving skills among younger generations and teaching traditional techniques to urban indigenous youth. He often says: "The carving knife is my pen, wood is my canvas."

**Yuma Taru (尤瑪‧達陸)** emerged as a key promoter of Atayal weaving art. Beginning in the 1990s, she devoted herself to revitalizing traditional weaving while pushing textile art into contemporary realms. Her "Wild Tung Workshop" became an important base for Atayal weaving culture.

Yuma's textile works maintain traditional techniques while boldly experimenting with new color and pattern combinations. Her large-scale textile installation "Words of Ancestors" exhibited at the 2010 Taipei Biennial introduced international art circles to the contemporary charm of Taiwanese indigenous weaving.

### Second-Generation Breakthrough Artists

**Rahic Talif (拉黑子‧達立夫)** is an Amis contemporary artist whose work boldly challenges boundaries between tradition and modernity. In 2019, he represented Taiwan at the Venice Biennale, becoming the first indigenous artist to represent Taiwan.

Rahic's "Wishing/Originating" series explores the complexity of indigenous identity. He creates large installations using driftwood, discarded materials, and modern components, reflecting indigenous conditions in modern society. Driftwood symbolizes uprooted indigenous peoples while simultaneously demonstrating tenacious vitality.

His creative methods fuse performance art, installation art, and video art, breaking traditional media limitations. At the Venice Biennale, his work "If You Stand at the Center of the World and Shout Love" combined traditional Amis songs with contemporary video technology, creating stunning audiovisual experiences.

**An Sheng-hui (安聖惠)** is a Paiwan ceramicist whose work elevates traditional pottery crafts to modern ceramic art heights. Her pieces combine traditional Paiwan pottery forms and firing techniques with greater freedom and experimentation in form and concept.

An's "Land Memory" series uses clay to record Paiwan tribal spaces and living memories. She collects soil from different tribes, mixing and firing them into ceramics, allowing each piece to carry the history and memory of specific places.

### Third-Generation Innovators

**Eleng Luluan (峨冷‧魯魯安)** is a Rukai curator and artist dedicated to promoting indigenous contemporary art development. Her curated "Art Festival on the Slope" became an important platform for indigenous art, showcasing new generations of indigenous artistic energy.

Eleng's curatorial philosophy emphasizes "de-marginalization." She believes indigenous art should not be viewed as "special" or "other" but as an integral component of Taiwan's contemporary art. Her exhibition "So That's How It Is" re-examined the development trajectory of Taiwanese indigenous art, receiving high praise from art circles.

**Lin Jie-wen (林介文)** represents younger-generation Amis artists extensively utilizing new media technology. His VR work "Amis Ocean" immerses viewers in Amis maritime culture, experiencing traditional fishing and hunting life.

Lin's work reflects digital-generation indigenous cultural experience. He states: "Our generation of indigenous people grew up in the digital world from childhood. Creating with digital media is natural. The key is using modern technology to convey ancient wisdom."

## Contemporary Issues and Cultural Dialogue

### Identity Exploration

Indigenous contemporary artists face the core issue of identity. Most grew up in Han-dominated urban societies, potentially experiencing disconnection or ambiguity in their relationship with traditional culture. How to reconnect with cultural roots while establishing contemporary identity remains an important theme in many artists' work.

**Etan Pavavalung (伊誕‧巴瓦瓦隆)**'s photography series "Who Are We" documents urban indigenous lifestyles. He photographs indigenous workers, students, and office workers in Taipei, showing their efforts to maintain cultural identity within urban life.

These photographs reveal office workers wearing suits but traditional necklaces, students working in convenience stores who speak tribal languages, and families living in apartments while continuing traditional ceremonies. These images reflect the complexity and diversity of contemporary indigenous identity.

### Land and Environmental Issues

Land constitutes the foundation of indigenous culture and an important theme in contemporary artistic creation. Many artists express concerns about land development and environmental destruction through their work, advocating for indigenous land rights.

**Siki Sufin (希巨‧蘇飛)**, an Atayal artist, created the large-scale landscape work "Mother's Call" exhibited in Jianshi Township, Hsinchu County. The work uses local stones to create enormous spiral structures symbolizing Mother Earth's embrace. Traditional plants surrounding the work recreate Atayal botanical knowledge.

This piece functions not only as aesthetic creation but also as a vehicle for environmental education and cultural transmission. Viewers learn Atayal ecological wisdom while appreciating the artwork.

**Adaw Palaf Langasan (阿道‧巴辣夫‧冉而山)**, a Puyuma artist, created the video work "Songs of Nuclear Waste" documenting Tao resistance to nuclear waste storage on Orchid Island. In the work, Tao elders use traditional songs to express anger and sorrow about environmental pollution.

These creations demonstrate indigenous artists' sense of social responsibility — they are not merely aesthetic creators but guardians of culture and environment.

### Gender and Family Memory

In recent years, female indigenous artists' voices have become increasingly important. They explore gender roles and family inheritance within indigenous culture from female and maternal perspectives.

**Iyo Kacaw (伊佑‧噶照)** is an Amis female artist whose installation "Mother's Hands" recreates Amis women's living spaces. A giant stone mortar sits at the work's center, surrounded by various traditional living implements, symbolizing women's important position in cultural transmission.

Amis society is matrilineal, with women holding important family positions. Iyo's work reminds viewers that women's wisdom and contributions in indigenous culture are often overlooked and deserve rediscovery and respect.

**Wan-mao Lu Sen-bao (芫茂‧陸森寶)** is a Puyuma female artist whose fiber art piece "Grandmother's Stories" creates modern sculpture using traditional weaving techniques. Various plant fibers interweave into complex net-like structures symbolizing family memory transmission.

She explains: "Grandmother's stories are woven by hand — each knot is a memory, each thread a piece of history. My work makes these invisible stories visible."

## International Stage and Global Dialogue

### Venice Biennale Breakthrough

Rahic Talif's representation of Taiwan at the 2019 Venice Biennale marked an important breakthrough for Taiwanese indigenous art on the international stage. His exhibition "If You Stand at the Center of the World and Shout Love" received significant attention from international art circles.

This participation's significance extended beyond artistic achievement to cultural symbolism. An artist from Taiwan's indigenous tribal communities standing on the world's most important art stage, speaking for indigenous peoples — this was previously unimaginable.

Rahic's Venice work emphasized the concept of "empathy," inviting viewers to feel indigenous life experiences. He used the traditional Amis song "Elderly Drinking Song" as background audio, letting visitors from around the world hear Taiwanese indigenous voices.

### International Indigenous Art Networks

Taiwanese indigenous artists actively participate in international indigenous art networks, collaborating and exchanging with indigenous artists from Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and other nations. These transnational connections represent not only artistic exchange but also important components of indigenous rights movements.

The 2018 "Pacific Islander Indigenous Contemporary Art Exhibition" at Taipei Fine Arts Museum invited indigenous artists from Taiwan, Philippines, Indonesia, Australia, and other countries to exhibit together. This exhibition demonstrated both diversity and commonality within Austronesian cultures.

Non-indigenous artists like **Tong Yang-tze (董陽孜)** actively support indigenous art development, collaborating with numerous indigenous artists to promote cross-cultural dialogue. Such cross-ethnic collaborative models bring new perspectives and possibilities to Taiwan's art world.

## Market Development and Industrialization

### Art Market Opportunities and Challenges

Taiwanese indigenous contemporary art gradually gains market recognition but faces ongoing challenges. Traditional crafts have stable collector markets, but contemporary artworks still need greater market awareness.

Public museums like Kaohsiung Museum of Fine Arts and Taipei Fine Arts Museum actively collect indigenous contemporary artworks, establishing market standards. Private galleries and collectors also begin focusing on this field, though overall market scale remains limited.

An important trend involves discussions of "cultural authenticity." Collectors and curators increasingly value works' cultural backgrounds and creators' identity, presenting both opportunities and challenges for indigenous artists.

### Cultural Creative Industry Development

Indigenous art develops beyond pure art fields, combining with cultural creative industries to create diverse products and services.

The "Indigenous Cultural Creative Industry Cluster" established in Chenggong Township, Taitung County, brings together multiple indigenous artists and craftspeople to develop culturally distinctive creative products. Products include modernized textile accessories, lifestyle goods incorporating traditional patterns, and clothing designs with indigenous elements.

These industrialization attempts face balance issues between "commercialization" and "cultural authenticity." Maintaining cultural content while pursuing commercial development represents a crucial challenge for industry advancement.

Taiwanese indigenous contemporary art has traveled the path from margin to center over the past 30 years. Artists have proven through their creations that indigenous culture is not merely cultural heritage requiring protection but contemporary force capable of dialoguing with the entire world. Their works not only demonstrate indigenous cultural richness but also inject unique vitality into Taiwan's contemporary art. In our globalized era, these voices from tribal communities appear increasingly precious and important.
